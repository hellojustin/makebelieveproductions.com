# Brand Voice

This skill defines how Make Believe Productions sounds in writing. Read it before drafting any outward-facing content.

## Who we are

A studio that uses modern AI tools to build high-quality software at high speed. We care about craft — we want every detail to have been considered.

## Voice principles

### Calm confidence, not chest-thumping

We don't announce that we're the best. We don't compare ourselves to others. We don't say "we don't have that problem" or "the room gets quiet." Authority comes from the specificity of our observations and the quality of our thinking, not from claiming superiority.

Wrong: "We don't have that problem. And we're open-sourcing the reason why."
Right: "We've been thinking a lot about what happens to a codebase when you build quickly with AI."

Wrong: "Most teams struggle with this. We've solved it."
Right: "The question is whether you have a practice for picking them up."

### Inviting, not lecturing

We share what we've learned, not what others should do. We describe our approach and let the reader decide if it's useful. We never talk down to the audience — they're peers, often founders and technical leaders who have their own hard-won perspectives.

Wrong: "You need to treat hygiene as a continuous practice."
Right: "We treat codebase hygiene as a continuous practice, not a periodic chore."

### Generous, not possessive

When we open-source something or share an idea, we do it with genuine openness. We acknowledge that others will adapt, disagree, or improve on what we've done, and we're comfortable with that.

Wrong: "Use our system and your codebase will be clean."
Right: "They're opinionated — they reflect how we think about code quality and what we've found works at the pace we operate. Your team may use them differently, adapt them, or disagree with some of the choices. That's fine."

### Specific and concrete, not abstract

We show rather than tell. Instead of saying "AI can create problems," we describe the specific problem: "The agent created a utility it didn't know already existed." Instead of saying "this makes teams hesitate," we describe the hesitation: "When you're not sure what's tested, you don't refactor."

Wrong: "Technical debt accumulates and slows teams down."
Right: "Each small uncertainty adds drag, and at some point the drag outweighs the speed."

### Simple language, not jargon

We use plain words. We don't lean on industry jargon to sound credible. When we do use a technical term, we make sure the surrounding context gives it meaning. A founder who isn't deeply technical should be able to follow every sentence.

Wrong: "Our automated CI/CD hygiene pipeline leverages static analysis to ensure codebase maintainability."
Right: "We've automated most of this into a set of Cursor skills — small, composable instructions that teach the AI agent to clean up after itself."

## Sentence and paragraph style

**Short paragraphs.** Rarely more than three sentences. Often one or two. White space is part of the voice — it gives ideas room to breathe.

**Varied rhythm.** Alternate between longer, flowing sentences and short declarative ones. The short ones land harder because the long ones came first.

Example of rhythm:
"Not the first week — the first week is wonderful. You describe what you want, the agent builds it, and the thing works. You ship it. You do it again. The pace feels like a superpower."

**Dashes for asides.** Use em dashes to add context or qualification mid-sentence. This keeps the main thought moving while adding nuance. "We treat codebase hygiene the way we treat any other aspect of craft — as a continuous practice, not a periodic chore."

**No exclamation marks.**

**No emoji in long-form writing.** Social posts can use them sparingly if the platform expects it, but blog posts and documentation never.

## Structural habits

**Lead with curiosity, not conclusions.** Open a piece with an observation or question, not a thesis statement. Let the reader arrive at the conclusion with you.

Wrong opening: "Codebase hygiene is essential for fast-moving teams."
Right opening: "We've been thinking a lot about what happens to a codebase when you build quickly with AI."

**Thesis at the end, not the beginning.** Build toward the central idea rather than opening with it. The reader should arrive at the conclusion alongside us, not be handed it up front.

**Minimal headers.** Use section breaks when the topic genuinely shifts, not as formatting decoration. A 400-word post needs two or three headers at most.

**No bullet-point lists in essays.** If something wants to be a list, rewrite it as prose or a sequence of short sentences. Lists are for documentation, not for storytelling.

## What we never do

- **Never denigrate other tools, teams, or approaches.** We talk about what we do, not what others do wrong.
- **Never use "vibe coding" to describe our work.** We do high-craft development at high speed. The term "vibe coding" implies carelessness, which is the opposite of how we work.
- **Never use urgency or fear as a motivator.** We don't say "if you don't do this, your codebase will collapse." We describe what we've found works and why we think it matters.
- **Never use superlatives.** No "best," "fastest," "most powerful," "revolutionary." These words mean nothing and signal insecurity.
- **Never use the word "leverage" as a verb.** Or "utilize." Just say "use."
- **Never say "game-changer," "unlock," "supercharge," "next-level," or "10x."** These are empty calories.

## The feeling we want to leave

After reading something we've written, the reader should feel like they just had a thoughtful conversation with someone who knows their craft and isn't trying to sell them anything.
